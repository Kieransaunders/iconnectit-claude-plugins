# Channels — inbound vs outbound, and a playbook each

## Inbound vs outbound

**Outbound** = you go to the customer: paid clicks, impressions, sponsored
reviews, banner ads, bought placements. Has a direct cost and generally lower
conversion than inbound because it interrupts. Useful with budget and a reason;
rarely necessary in the early stages of a plugin business.

**Inbound** = the customer comes to you: SEO, blog/educational content, docs,
social, community, free resources. Costs time not budget, compounds over time,
and converts better because it earns attention rather than buying it. **Default
to inbound.** The best inbound doesn't read as marketing — it's genuinely useful
content that happens to feature the product.

Rule of thumb: an eBook/guide a user chooses to download is inbound; a banner
ad slapped in front of everyone is outbound.

## Anchoring content to the raw bits

Every piece of content should trace to one raw bit (see SKILL.md):

- **Pain-point** → case studies, problem/solution tutorials, email sequences,
  webinars.
- **Experience** → how-to blog posts, landing pages, docs, FAQs, video
  walkthroughs.
- **Unique approach** → opinionated/thought-leadership posts, interviews,
  podcast appearances, quotable social posts.

Example, a caching plugin (raw bit = experience): a "make your WordPress site
faster" blog post; a setup walkthrough video; a partner-usage podcast clip; a
speed-optimisation guide behind an opt-in that lists the plugin as one option; a
monthly webinar on why speed matters. One raw bit → many assets.

## Channel playbooks (inbound-first)

**Content marketing / blog** — target real search queries tied to the
pain-point; teach, don't pitch; mention the product as the natural tool. Needs
sustained cadence to pay off (see `product-website.md` on the blog decision).

**SEO** — keyword in title + short description (on .org and site); flat site
structure; helpful long-form content; internal links from posts to the
buy/features pages; build install count, reviews, and "tested up to" freshness
for .org ranking.

**Social media** — show the product working (clips, before/after), share the
thought-leadership angle, engage in WordPress communities rather than broadcast.
Pick the 1–2 platforms where the ICP actually is (often LinkedIn + X for B2B
WordPress; relevant Facebook/Reddit/Slack communities).

**Email** — own the relationship the algorithms don't control. Capture leads
with a useful opt-in, segment by lifecycle/interest, automate welcome,
free→paid, and abandonment sequences. (Details in `product-website.md`.)

**Affiliate** — let others sell for you on commission; low fixed cost, scales
with the product. Good once there's a paid product and some proof it converts.

**Influencer / community** — get the product in front of trusted WordPress
voices (reviews, tutorials, podcast guesting). High trust transfer; pursue once
the product and copy are solid.

**Paid / SEM (outbound)** — only with budget and a clear target segment; send
clicks to a dedicated, conversion-focused landing page, not the homepage; track
with UTMs and measure cost per acquisition against CLV. Don't start here.

## Choosing channels for a one-person / small operation

Concentrate. Pick the smallest set that reaches the ICP and can be sustained:
usually a strong .org listing + a focused product site + email capture + one
content/social channel done consistently. Breadth without consistency loses to
depth on a few channels. Use the impact-vs-effort matrix
(`prioritisation.md`) to choose.
