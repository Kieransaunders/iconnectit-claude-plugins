# Step 1 — Minimum Viable Content (MVC) assets

The MVC assets are the written foundation every other channel reuses (the .org
page, the website, emails, social). Produce them as finished copy, not prompts.
Each asset below has a template and a quality bar. Anchor every asset to one of
the three raw bits — pain-point, experience, or unique approach.

## Quality checklist (apply to every asset before handing back)

- Does the copy talk directly to the buyer/user, in their words, about their
  problem — not the founder's cleverness?
- Benefit before feature. A feature only earns its place once the benefit is
  stated ("X so that you can Y").
- Specific over generic. Name the segment, the task, the saved time, the number.
- No typos, no jargon the buyer wouldn't use, no unclear sentences.
- Up to date with the current version of the product.
- UK English, GBP, GDPR-aware by default.

---

## Tagline (1 line)

One distinctive line that says what it is and why it's different. Avoid
category-generic ("The best caching plugin"). Lead with the outcome or the
unique angle.

**Template:** `[Outcome / unique angle] for [specific WordPress audience].`

**Examples:**
- `Real-time Airtable data in your WordPress pages — no refresh, no rebuild.`
- `The booking plugin that doesn't charge per booking.`

## Short description (≤150 characters)

Used on .org and in search snippets. Front-load the keyword the buyer searches
for, then the benefit. Count the characters.

**Template:** `[Keyword / category] that [primary benefit] for [audience].`

## Long description (benefit-led)

A page explaining what the product does, organised by what the user gains.
Structure:

1. One-paragraph hook restating the pain-point and the relief.
2. 3–6 benefit blocks: each is a bold benefit headline + 1–2 sentences, with the
   feature named as the *how*.
3. Who it's for (and, honestly, who it's not for — builds trust).
4. Social proof if available.
5. Clear CTA.

## Top concerns / objections (5 minimum)

List the real questions and hesitations a prospect has before installing or
buying, and answer each in 2–3 sentences. Mine these from support tickets,
reviews, and sales chats if available. These feed the .org FAQ and the website
FAQ directly. Typical themes: compatibility, performance impact, data/privacy,
pricing/licensing, migration/lock-in, support quality.

## Screenshots (brief + captions)

You can't take the screenshots, so produce: (a) a shot list of the 4–8 views
that best prove the product works (the "aha" moments, not the settings screen),
and (b) a one-line annotated caption for each, written to sell the benefit shown.
Captions are reused verbatim on .org and the site.

## Five cornerstone pages

The reusable core of the docs/blog. Write these as finished pages:

1. **Setup** — install to first value, step by step.
2. **How to use it (the demo)** — the main workflow end to end; this is the
   "show me it working" page.
3–5. **Three concrete use cases** — each a real scenario for a real segment
   ("How [persona] uses [product] to [outcome]"). Use cases convert far better
   than feature lists because the reader sees themselves.

## About page (customer-first)

Write the About page about the *customer's* world and why the product exists for
them — the founder/company story is the supporting act, not the headline. End
with the mission and a CTA. Avoid the "we were founded in…" opener.

## Ideal Customer Profile (ICP)

A short, shared, documented definition of exactly who the product is for. Keep
it usable, not academic:

- **Who they are** — role, business type/size, WordPress setup.
- **The job they're trying to do** and the pain when they can't.
- **Triggers** — what makes them go looking for a solution now.
- **Where they hang out** — channels/communities (feeds `channels.md`).
- **Objections** — what makes them hesitate (feeds the objections asset).
- **Anti-profile** — who it's *not* for, so copy can stay sharp.

## Storage convention

Keep all MVC assets together (the user's own "Content Assets" folder, or one
output file per asset with clear names). They are the raw material for Steps 2–4
— never re-derive copy that already exists; reuse and adapt it.
